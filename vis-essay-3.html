The Indian Rights Association (IRA) was an activist group founded by non-Indians in 1882. They believed that the Bureau of Indian Affairs was holding Indians back from this possible future by providing too much paternalistic support and advocated for its abolition. They also believed that Christianity, education, and farming on individually allotted lands would civilize Indians, with their main goal being the extension of citizenship to Indians so that Indians would cease exist[ing] as a man, apart from other men.1 Prior to the Indian Citizenship Act of 1924, which granted U.S. citizenship to all Indians, Indians could still become citizens by marrying whites, serving in the military, or accepting allotments, but the IRA sought something more comprehensive.

Although the IRA is known for advocating for Indian citizenship, attention has not been paid to how much their understanding of citizenship or what it would mean for Indians changed over time. The rights and responsibilities that people associate with citizenship are not stable, nor is the understanding of how much citizenship is a matter of local versus national belonging.

To examine how the IRA thought about citizenship, I am using the wordVectors package on the organizations annual reports for every year from 1883 to 1927. I created two models by splitting the reports into two sections: 1883-1904 and 1905-1927. 1905 marked the end of organization founder Herbert Welshs leadership, and this also happens to split the available reports almost equally in half.

Using the closest_to function on the words citizenship, citizen, and citizens on the top 5000 words in each of the two models gives the following lists of 30 words related to citizenship.

The words that I typically associate with citizenship are words like responsibilities, privileges, rights, freedom, and duties. What stands out right away is that only rights is included in the list for 1883-1904 while all of these words are in the list for 1905-1927 (additionally, rights has a higher score there than in the first list). This information, paired with the fact that ownership has a very high score in the first list but does not appear in the second list, indicates a changing notion of Indian citizenship. It seems that the IRA moved away their initial argument that citizenship needed to be paired with individual ownership of allotted land and replaced this with a definition of Indian citizenship rooted primarily in fulfilling responsibilities. That responsibilities has a higher score than rights or privileges shows that they were thinking more about what Indians needed to do as citizens rather than the benefits that Indians would derive from being citizens. These rhetorical changes were not necessarily isolated to questions surrounding Indian citizenship; the new intense association between citizenship and responsibilities may have been tied to the impact of World War I.2 More research is necessary to understand if the changes in how the IRA talked about citizenship had more to do with these broad, national changes or with something more specific like growing disillusion with allotment, but the stark contrast between the two lists demonstrates that citizenship took on new meaning for the IRA in the twentieth century.
